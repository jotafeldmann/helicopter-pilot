## Welcome

Welcome, Pilot-Soldier 🪖🚁

So you wanna take to the skies, huh? This guide is your ticket out of the mud and into the rotor-blade rumble of Battlefield 6.

Whether you’re a ground grunt looking to level up or a seasoned sky menace, this intro is your runway. Ready to spin those blades? Let’s fly.

### What is this guide about?

This guide is a hope for regular players that want to be strategic — not aces.  
If you want to be at least a little useful and not just treat helicopters as taxis, this is for you.

And NO, there is no keymap, hover assist, yada yada, this stuff you can find anywhere else. Tip: Google.

The reality is: playing online is 99% of the time like flying a helicopter full of ducks — nobody talks, everyone shoots at random things, and someone will always jump out at the worst possible moment.

As the old helicopter saying goes:

> “Flying a helicopter is like juggling while people are throwing rocks at you.”

…But that’s also what makes it so much fun.

I’ve got a thing or two to share with those who want to get more out of choppers in this classic series.

If you’re a skilled pilot… **GET OUT OF MY CHOPPA!** 🫡🚁

## Updates

## Updates for version 1.2.2.0

This update included actual helicopter gameplay changes.

### What changed

- Improved helicopter landing stability, reducing surface sliding on touchdown.
- Increased UH-79 health by 20% in all multiplayer modes except Battle Royale.
- Reduced the speed of Attack Helicopter Aim-Guided Missiles.
- Scout helicopter passengers can no longer use Engineer rocket launchers.
- Fixed an issue where scout helicopter tracers could appear behind the barrel.
- AI was improved when using helicopter flares and anti-air behavior was also adjusted.

### Impact

This was not just a cosmetic patch.  
It made helicopters more stable when landing, buffed the UH-79’s survivability, reduced passenger abuse on scout helis, and slightly toned down guided missile pressure from attack helicopters.

## Updates for version 1.1.1.5

This patch was much smaller for helicopters, but it fixed one major issue.

### What changed

- Fixed an issue where Lock-Guided Missiles were not being properly countered by enemy countermeasures such as flares.

### Impact

This did not directly rebalance helicopter handling, health, or weapons.  
What it did was restore proper flare interaction against lock-guided missiles, which was a major survivability fix for air vehicles.

## Doctrine

### Basic assumptions for multiplayer games

- Be realistic — most players on the internet won't fully coordinate. Expect limited comms and play accordingly.
- You’re on your own, but not alone — teammates exist; use them. Stay behind friendly infantry and AA vehicles and you’ll be much safer.
- Check the map & read behavior — watch spawn points, objective pushes, and where teammates cluster before committing.
- Don’t outpace your team — if you fly too far ahead of infantry/vehicles you lose support and become an easy target.
- Helicopters are a shared resource — seats, ammo, and flares are limited; assume someone else will take the bird at some point.
- Expect early AA and jets — assume lock-ons and enemy aircraft will show up; plan altitude and flares accordingly.
- Minimal coordination = more scouting — spot and mark targets yourself instead of waiting for pings.
- Adapt to team skill & ticket state — if your team is weak or low on tickets, play safer and prioritize objectives over heroics.
- Assume visibility draws fire — being obvious = being shot at; use terrain, buildings, and timing to stay hidden.
- Have simple fallback plans — identify safe intermediate bases or extraction routes before you commit.

## Basics

### Basic Tips for Rookie Pilots

- Know your controls — before you take off, get comfy with the sticks and buttons. A good pilot doesn’t fumble mid-dogfight.
- Use hover assist — let the chopper do some of the balancing work so you can focus on blasting stuff.
- Stay high, stay smart — altitude is your best buddy. Keep above the chaos when you can.
- Don’t get too cozy with the ground — that’s where rockets, AA guns, and bad decisions live.
- Spawn early — helicopters are limited, and every soldier wants a seat. Be quick.
- Switch camera views — cockpit for precision, chase for keeping an eye on incoming trouble.
- Tweak your settings — sensitivity and dead zones can make the difference between smooth flying and doing barrel rolls into a tree.
- Respect the lock-on beep — when it starts screaming, so should your evasive maneuvers.
- Some maps have automatic anti-aircraft emplaced weapons: use as a resource when running from fools or when getting close to enemy's main base
- Did it take a lot of damage and still survive? Just return to base (the big "H" in the ground) and give some time, the heli will recover. If you're feeling adventurous, join as an engineer, land the heli, and repair it.

**HARD PERSONAL TIP:** We die with the heli — When Im playing like a pilot Ill die with my steel dragon. No return. No Bail out.

## Helicopter: Transport

### Not a taxi

In my experience, the true power of transport helicopters isn’t their guns… it’s their unique ability to be a mobile spawn point, moving and bringing your team to the fight along the map, to advance for farthest bases or defend the closest ones.

### tl;dr;

In case of doubt, keep roaming the bases closer to your HQ, spawning teammates, and taking back/holding the closest bases.

- Think like a mobile spawn point — the true power of transport helicopters isn’t their guns… it’s their ability to keep your team in the fight.
- Your main job is mobility — transport helicopters aren’t attack birds. Your strength is moving troops fast and safely where they need to be.
- Pick smart drop zones — don’t land in the middle of the objective; drop troops just outside with good cover and line of sight.
- Use hover carefully — hover low and steady for a quick drop, but don’t stay in one place too long. You’re a big, easy target.
- Fly under radar — staying low under 30 meters during approach makes you harder to lock on and less visible to jets and AA.
- Avoid the obvious path — approach objectives from the sides or behind, not straight from spawn. Predictable routes get you killed.
- Keep moving — after a drop, don’t linger. Circle, reposition, or go pick up more troops. A stationary transport is a free kill.
- Use terrain to mask — hills, buildings, and elevation can shield your bird from AA and snipers during approach.
- Don’t play hero — you’re a flying bus, not a gunship. Focus on insertions, not dogfights.
- Save flares for the right moment — pop them after the lock-on warning turns into a launch, not before. Timing matters.
- Support the frontline — ferry squads quickly to secondary objectives or reinforce critical fights. Good logistics win battles.
- Be predictable to your team, not your enemies — use consistent rally routes so allies know where to hop in, but vary your approach to confuse enemies.

## Helicopter: Attack helicopter

### The best defense is a good offense (mostly)

but

> “The defensive form of war is intrinsically stronger than the offensive.  
> We maintain … that the advantage of the defensive is very great, and that it increases with the size of the force and the duration of the war.  
> The defensive is the stronger form of conducting war, the attack the more effective form, and the latter the aim of the former.  
> The strength of the defensive does not lie in the passive state itself, but in the counterattack which is prepared by it.”
>
> — Carl von Clausewitz, *On War* (1832)

- Role clarity: you’re a tank/vehicle killer, not an invincible god — focus on disabling armor and denying map control near spawn areas; leave long-range air duels to dedicated jets and far away tanks for ally tanks.
- **HARD PERSONAL TIP:** Infantry its not your priority — Unless they are a group along the way or taking some base.
- Pick your loadout for the job — use anti-armor rockets/ guided missiles and a reliable cannon. Balance between pure AT and some AoE for infantry.
- Approach from unexpected angles — attack tanks from flanks or rear where their armor and crew visibility is weaker. Don’t charge straight down the main avenue.
- Use cover and pop-shooting — hug terrain, buildings, and ridges; expose briefly to fire, then duck back to avoid return fire or lock-ons.
- Position near friendly AA / SAMs — operate where ground-based anti-air and friendly jets can help; don’t be the lone helicopter near the spawn area.
- Engage with target priority — take out rocket-armed vehicles and AA first, then focus tanks and armor. Ignoring the AA will get you killed fast.
- Use the gunner well — coordinate target calls with your gunner; they should focus infantry and soft targets while you handle hardened vehicles.
- Keep moving — don’t hover in the open — constant motion makes lock-ons harder and reduces the chance of being swarmed by jets or guided rockets.
- Conserve defensive countermeasures — save flares/chaff for actual missile launches; wasting them early leaves you vulnerable when it matters.
- Manage ammo and cooling — track missile and cannon ammo; reload or retreat before you run dry so you’re not helpless at a critical moment.
- Deny spawning routes — hover or patrol just outside spawn-adjacent objectives to intercept armored pushes and force enemies into cover.
- Use altitude smartly near bases — low enough to be under jet radar but high enough to see and engage tanks; adjust to the map and AA threats.
- Communicate with your team — call out armor locations, request AA support, and ping safe LZs for reinforcements or extraction.
- Have an escape plan — always know a retreat route—toward friendly AA, behind terrain, or to a safe resupply—so you can live to fight another pass.
- Don’t chase kills into traps — a burned-out tank area can be bait with nearby AA or anti-air infantry waiting; take a moment to scan before diving.

### tl;dr;

Hit and Run, but not too far from home.

## Round strategy: early game

### First move

- Climb high or go low — know your map — your altitude strategy depends on the terrain and layout.
- Fly low at the start — as a rule of thumb, stay under 30 meters right after takeoff. This makes it harder for enemy jets and lock-on launchers to target you.
- Use the terrain — buildings, hills, and cover will break enemy line of sight, buying you precious seconds.
- Climb only when safe — once the skies are clearer or you’ve scoped out enemy AA, then rise for a better tactical view.
- Stay unpredictable — mix your altitude changes during flight to throw off locks and jet pilots.

### First objective

- Don’t be audacious — play it safe: pick the intermediate base that you can realistically capture with the helicopter and the time available without direct contact
- Choose a secure insertion point — land or hover far enough from enemy concentrations to avoid direct contact, but close enough to reach the objective quickly.
- Use hover to arrive first — use your hover capability to insert before ground vehicles and infantry, giving your team the initial advantage.
- Know the window you need — only pick bases you can take within the time you’ll be exposed; don’t gamble on long, risky holds.
- Keep an escape route ready — pick landing spots with clear egress so you can lift off if AA or heavy resistance shows up.
- Avoid hot LZs — if the base is already heavily contested, move to a safer nearby spot and support from distance instead of crashing the landing.
- Prioritize safety over heroics — a reliable, repeatable capture sets your team up better than a flashy one-off that gets you blown up.

## Round strategy: mid to late game

- Fly low and wide — instead of going straight through the middle of the battlefield, circle around the edges of the map. Staying under radar and avoiding the main AA zones will keep you alive longer.
- Hunt for key forward bases — focus on capturing or supporting the capture of bases closer to the enemy’s main spawn. This puts pressure on their logistics and limits their options.
- Use the terrain as cover — hills, valleys, and structures can hide your approach, letting you get close without lighting up every lock-on on the map.
- Pick soft objectives first — hit less-defended points behind the frontline. They’re easier to take and can destabilize the enemy’s defense.
- Keep your retreat path clear — if things get hot, you should always have a clean escape route back toward friendly AA and support.
- Don’t hover in contested areas — drop off troops or provide a quick strike, then pull back and reposition. Staying put is asking for a rocket to the face.
- Chain objectives — move from one base to another quickly, keeping momentum. The more pressure you apply from the sides, the less coordinated the enemy becomes.
- Avoid the main meat grinder — let the tanks and infantry trade in the center while you quietly take ground around them.
- Watch for enemy air patrols — jets and attack choppers often sweep the backlines mid-game. Anticipate their routes and hug terrain to avoid being spotted.
- Support your team’s advance — once a flank is open, use your mobility to reinforce pushes or cut off enemy reinforcements.

## A word about jets

Jets deserve respect.

Right now, in Battlefield 6, they feel harder to use well than many players expect, but they are still one of the biggest threats to helicopters simply because of speed, angle of attack, and how fast they can appear, leave, and come back.

For helicopter players, the main thing to understand is this:

**you do not beat jets by chasing jets.**

You beat them by:
- staying low,
- using terrain,
- avoiding obvious flight paths,
- and never hanging in the open longer than necessary.

If a jet pilot knows where you are and has a clean line on you, the problem usually started earlier, with your positioning.

Maps also feel a bit tight for air play, which means jet contact happens fast and often with little warning.  
That makes route discipline even more important.

Always keep one eye on the radar and one eye on the sky.  
A jet is often dangerous before you even see it clearly, so the sooner you notice movement, direction, or a possible attack line, the better your chance to survive the first pass.

What gives a jet a clear advantage over you is also part of its weakness.

Its speed, attack line, and need to commit to a pass make it dangerous, but they also make it more predictable once the first attack begins.

So the goal is not to panic and start throwing the helicopter around like crazy.

The goal is to **survive the first hits, dodge the first pass, and stay alive long enough for the jet to continue its path**.

Once the jet commits, gravity, speed, and angle usually carry it forward.  
That is your moment.

If you stay calm, keep control, and do not overreact, you can often let the jet overshoot, watch it follow its line, and then return fire while hovering more steadily and with better aim.

In other words:

**the jet wins the entry.  
You win the recovery window.**

For regular helicopter pilots, the rule is simple:

- do not fly high unless there is a reason,
- do not hover where the sky is wide open,
- do not repeat the same approach too often,
- survive the first pass,
- and punish the overshoot if the chance appears.

If you are flying transport, jets are one more reason to think like a moving spawn point instead of a hero vehicle.  
If you are flying attack helicopter, jets are one more reason to make short attack windows, disappear again, and stay ready for the return pass.

Jets are not just another enemy vehicle.  
They are the reminder that the sky is never really yours.
