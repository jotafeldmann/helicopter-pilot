# Helicopter Pilot

Silly findings for digital weekend helicopter pilots.

## About this README

This is a small collection of notes, impressions, and practical ideas for people who fly helicopters for fun, not for glory.

It is not meant to be a hardcore sim manual.  
It is more like a field notebook for regular players who enjoy transport runs, risky insertions, awkward landings, and the occasional miracle escape with 3% health.

If you like figuring things out by flying, crashing, adjusting, and trying again, this is for you.

## Why helicopters?

Helicopters sit in that weird space between control and panic.

They are not as stable as ground vehicles.  
They are not as fast or untouchable as jets.  
And they are almost never as safe as they look from the ground.

That is exactly why they are fun.

A good helicopter run feels less like domination and more like problem solving:  
where to enter, when to leave, how much risk to take, and how to stay useful without becoming a free kill.

## Other helicopter games and inspirations

Part of the fun is that Battlefield helicopters sit somewhere between arcade action and sim habits.

On one side, there is **Arma 3**, which leaned harder into military sandbox flying and later expanded that with its helicopter-focused content.  
That side of the genre makes you think more about cargo, insertion, route discipline, and surviving through procedure instead of pure aggression.

On another side, there is **Microsoft Flight Simulator**, which eventually added helicopters and gave players a very different kind of satisfaction:  
less combat, more control, patience, hovering, approach discipline, and respect for how unstable rotary flight can feel when you stop treating it like a videogame toy.

And before all that, there were older classics and odd little ancestors across PC and home systems:  
**Gunship**, **Gunship 2000**, and **Gunship!** on PC;  
**Choplifter!** on early home computers and Atari-era platforms;  
and plenty of smaller action games where helicopters were less about realism and more about style, rescue, attack runs, and surviving impossible odds.

That long history is part of why helicopter gameplay still feels so appealing.  
Sometimes you want systems, trim, and precision.  
Sometimes you want noise, rockets, and bad decisions.  
Good helicopter games usually give you a bit of both.

## What this is really about

This README is for the regular pilot.

Not the elite pilot.  
Not the montage pilot.  
Not the guy who somehow reverse-rolls through three missiles and lands on a radio tower.

Just the normal pilot who wants to be useful.

That usually means:
- staying alive longer,
- choosing safer routes,
- helping your team move,
- avoiding greedy fights,
- and learning when not to push.

## Pilot philosophy

A few simple ideas show up again and again:

- Do not confuse mobility with invincibility.
- A helicopter is strongest when it chooses the fight.
- Terrain is often better protection than speed.
- A safe extraction is usually worth more than one extra kill.
- Transport helicopters win games quietly.
- Attack helicopters should solve problems, not chase highlights.
- If the situation feels messy, it probably already is.

## Closing note

I like helicopter games because they reward a weird mix of calm, stubbornness, and improvisation.

You can see that across Battlefield, Arma, Flight Simulator, and even the old-school helicopter games from earlier PC and console eras.  
Different tone, different complexity, same basic appeal:  
lift off, commit, adapt, survive.

That is the whole thing.
